•TOURIST GUIDE•

Japan has a history of 2,000 years. Its name is a combination of two ideograms, meaning "sun" and "beginning". It consists of four large islands, Hokkaido, Shikoku, Kyushu and Honshu, which are also accompanied by thousands of smaller ones (to be precise, the Japanese archipelago consists of 6,852 islands). Its largest city and capital is Tokyo, which together with the metropolitan area has more than 30 million inhabitants. In total, the country has a population, based on statistics for 2010, 127,360,000. Japan has the second largest economy in the world, by nominal GDP (although recent Chinese government figures suggest that China is now the second largest) and the third largest in terms of purchasing power. It is also the fourth largest exporter in the world and the fifth largest importer. Japan is especially known for its culture. In Japan, the No theater was developed, a word meaning "performance, performance, drama". The actors perform it with slow and religious movements. Many first-time visitors to Japan are often surprised to learn that as one of the most advanced industrial countries in the world, this relatively small Asian country also has a rich and fascinating history dating back thousands of years. Indeed, long before the construction of many of Europe's most impressive cathedrals, the Japanese Shinto and Buddhist temples were already established, painting pilgrims and patrons for their often elaborate designs and decorations. At the same time, the country had already perfected the skills and occupations that would put it on the path to wealth, from porcelain and ceramics to textiles such as silk. Much of this rich tradition has been preserved (or rebuilt), despite wars and natural disasters, and these days, a visit to Japan is an unforgettable adventure.

Mount Fuji

Without a doubt the most recognizable landmark of Japan, the majestic Mount Fuji (Fuji-san) is also the highest mountain in the country, rising 3,776 meters above an otherwise spacious landscape to the south and east, tall enough to seen from Tokyo more than 100 kilometers away.

Imperial Palace

Tokyo's most famous landmark, the Imperial Palace, with its beautiful 17th-century parks surrounded by walls and moats, is a must-see when visiting the nation's capital. Do not shy away from the fact that the majority of the palace is closed to the public (still used by the imperial family), as there is still enough space to see only on foot. In addition to the very nice view of the palace from many points in the surrounding park - including the famous Nijubashi Bridge, or "double bridge", so named for its watery reflection - guests are allowed in the East Garden Higashi-Gyoen, one of the few spaces that are open to the public.

Hiroshima Peace Park

While the horror of the atomic bombing of Hiroshima in August 1945 needless to say, much can be said about the incredible efforts made by this vibrant city to honor the many victims of the world's first and perhaps most important nuclear attack. the symbol of lasting peace Hiroshima has since become. Visited by more than one million people each year, many from abroad, Hiroshima Heiwa Kinen Kōen (Hiroshima Heiwa Kinen Kōen) is at the center of the atomic explosion in what was once a bustling part of the city and includes many important monuments, monuments and museums related to the events of that fateful day.

Historic Kyoto

One of Japan's most popular cities, the magnificent Kyoto, one of the few cities in the country that survived the devastation of World War II, attracts more than 10 million visitors a year to explore its beautiful old streets and architecture. Be sure to also visit Nijo Castle, a 17th-century fortress that has retained its original walls, towers, moat, beautiful gates and palace with magnificent interior design. Also worth a visit is the original Kyoto Imperial Palace (Kyoto-goso), built in 794 AD. and one of the most historic sights of the city. Finally, no visit to Kyoto is complete without spending time exploring Arashiyama Bamboo Grove, a beautiful tall bamboo area just a few minutes' walk from the city center.

The island of Ichukushima

Just a short ferry ride from mainland Hiroshima is Miyajima Island, famous worldwide as Shrine Island in Japan. Covering an area of ​​30 square kilometers in Hiroshima Bay, Miyajima is known as the home of Itsukushima Shrine, a Shinto temple dedicated to the daughters of the princess of the wind god Susanoo. Dating from the eighth century, the majority of the sanctuary buildings emerge from the waters of a small bay supported only by stakes. The effect of high tide is simply amazing, making these structures - including the famous Great Floating Gate (O-Torii) - appear to float in the water.

Temple City: Historic Nara

For centuries the center of Japanese culture, the beautiful pristine city of Nara has housed a large number of historic buildings as well as important national treasures and works of art. In addition to its many historic streets, the city boasts many important old temples, such as the magnificent seventh-century Temple of Kofuku-ji, best known of the Seven Great Temples of Nara, the magnificent eighth-century Todai-ji (Great Eastern Temple), known for the huge bronze statue of the Great Buddha (Daibutsu), built here in 749 AD.

Osaka Castle

Built in 1586 by a famous Japanese warrior and politician Toyotomi Hideyoshi, Osaka Castle (akasaka-jō) was then the largest and most important fortress in the country. Although many times destroyed and rebuilt, the current structure, built in 1931, remains true to the original. Attractions of a visit include the huge five-story main tower 42 meters long, built on an imposing 14-meter stone base and houses a series of screens describing the history of the castle and the city (be sure to visit the top floor for a great view in Osaka).

Chūbu-Sangaku National Park and the Japanese Alps

Japan boasts a number of exceptional natural beauty sites, many of which are national parks or, in some cases, UNESCO World Heritage Sites. One of the country’s most impressive parks is the Chūbu-Sangaku National Park in center of Honshu, which incorporates in its northern and central regions the mountain group collectively called the Hida Mountains, or Japanese Alps. The area includes some of the highest peaks in the country, including Hotaka at 3,190 meters and Yari at 3,180 meters. Of particular interest is the abundance of flora and fauna of the park, including the rare birds and antelopes of the mountains located at higher altitudes. The park's many hot springs attract visitors and lead to the development of various thermal baths and resorts, the most famous Kamikōchi.

Sanctuary of Atsuta, Nagoya

Atsuta Shrine, in the heart of Nagoya City, is the most important Shinto shrine in Japan, attracting more than five million visitors each year. Founded in the 1st century, this religious site is famous for its surviving imperial insignia, the "grass cutting sword" (kusanagi-no-tsurugi), one of three in the country. Also, its main shrine, Hongu, surrounded by a fenced wall, as well as the treasury with numerous works of art that include old and modern paintings, ceramics, jewelry and traditional masks. While in Nagoya, be sure to visit Nagoya Castle, a magnificent lawn complex built in 1612 that features a 48-meter-high main tower famous for its two gilded dolphins (shachi).

Fukuoka Castle and ancient festivals

One of the few surviving examples of old and majestic hill dwellings preferred by Shoguns and city governors, Fukuoka Castle (Fukuoka-jō) is one of the highlights of a visit to Fukuoka. Once part of a massive complex of approximately 47,000 square meters, this beautiful castle impresses with its size and location on a tall background overlooking the Naka River. Fukuoka is also known for its numerous events and festivals, especially the Hakata Gion Yamakasa, a famous 15-day festival that takes place every July, attracting millions of visitors from all over the country to its colorful parades, as well as her traditional tribes and costumes.
